1. What is "perplexity" and what score range flags as AI? Perplexity measures a language model's uncertainty, or "surprise," when predicting the next word in a sequence. Mathematically, it is the exponentiated average negative log-likelihood of the predicted words. AI-generated text is highly predictable and typically yields low perplexity scores (around 5 to 10 on standard benchmarks), whereas human writing is more creative and unpredictable, averaging higher scores around 20 to 50.
2. What is "burstiness" — exact formula and score ranges? Burstiness evaluates the variation in sentence length and structural complexity within a document. A standard formula used to quantify this is the coefficient of variation: B=(σ/μ)∗100, where σ is the standard deviation of sentence lengths and μ is the mean sentence length. Humans naturally write with high burstiness, mixing short, impactful sentences with long, complex ones. AI models tend to produce uniform, evenly spaced sentences, resulting in very low burstiness scores.
3. Do detectors analyze sentence-level or document-level signals? They analyze both. Detectors process text token-by-token to calculate sentence-level perplexity. They then aggregate these metrics across the entire text to evaluate document-level burstiness and overall statistical patterns. Tools like Originality.ai provide both a document-level confidence score and a sentence-by-sentence breakdown.
4. What token probability thresholds trigger a "likely AI" flag? Detectors do not usually rely on a single hard threshold for individual tokens; instead, they look at the aggregated conditional probability of tokens over a sequence. If a text consistently utilizes high-probability tokens, it results in a low overall perplexity. The detector's machine learning classifier then outputs a confidence score (e.g., "85% likely AI"), which represents the model's certainty that the pattern matches AI training data, rather than the exact percentage of AI-generated words.
5. What is the "watermarking" technique embedded in LLMs? Watermarking involves embedding subtle, tamper-proof statistical patterns into the text during the generation process. This can be done via lexical watermarking, which uses hashing algorithms to force specific word choices, or neural watermarking, which alters the probability distributions of the output tokens. While not fully deployed in all public models yet, specialized detectors can look for these exact distribution biases to definitively identify AI authorship.
6. Can semantic vector fingerprints be detected — and how? Yes, advanced detectors convert words and sentences into numerical vectors, known as embeddings, to capture semantic meaning and style. The detector compares the semantic vector of the input text to the vectors of known AI-generated examples. If the embeddings closely align with the statistical fingerprint of AI outputs, the system flags the text as machine-generated.
7. How are outputs from GPT-4, Claude, and Llama fingerprinted differently? Every LLM has a unique statistical fingerprint based on its specific architecture, training data, and decoding strategies. Machine learning classifiers in detectors are trained to recognize these specific patterns. Because of these differences, a detector trained heavily on GPT-3 outputs will often struggle to identify text from newer or different models, such as GPT-4 or Claude 3, resulting in false negatives.
